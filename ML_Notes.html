TF-IDF

Stands for Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency, used to reflect how important a word is to a document in a collection or corpus. This value increases proportionally to the number of times a word appears in the document and is offset by the number of documents containing the word.
For a given term (or n-gram) ttt in a document ddd, define the tern frequency tf(t,d)tf(t,d)tf(t,d) to be:

tf(t,d)=ft,d∑t′∈dft′,dtf(t,d) = \dfrac{f_{t,d}}{\sum_{t'\in d}f_{t',d}} tf(t,d)=∑t′∈d​ft′,d​ft,d​​

In other words, the count of the term in document over total term count in document, it has the log normalization of

f‾t,d=1+log⁡(ft,d)\overline{f}_{t,d}=1+\log(f_{t,d}) f​t,d​=1+log(ft,d​)

The inverse document requency is a measure of how rare the term is across all documents, it is logarithmically scaled inverse fraction of the documents that contain the word, obtained by dividing the total number of documents by the number of documents containing the term:

idf(t,D)=log⁡N∣{d∈D:t∈d}∣idf(t, D)=\log\dfrac{N}{|\{d \in D : t \in d\}|} idf(t,D)=log∣{d∈D:t∈d}∣N​

In python, use the TfidfVectorizer package:

from sklearn.feature_extraction.text import TfidfVectorizer

def tfidf_features(X_train, X_val, X_test):

  # min, max frequency arguments: integer for number of occurrences in document, float between 0
  # and 1 for percentage of occurrences
  tfidf_vectorizer = TfidfVectorizer(min_df=5, max_df=0.9, ngram_range=(1, 2), token_pattern='(\S+)')
  X_train = tfidf_vectorizer.fit_transform(X_train)
  X_val = tfidf_vectorizer.transform(X_val)
  X_test = tfidf_vectorizer.transform(X_test)

  return X_train, X_val, X)test, tfidf_vectorizer.vocabulary

Markov Property of Language Model

First, predict the probability of a sequence of words:
w=(w1,w2,w3,...,wk)\bm{w}=(w_1, w_2, w_3, ..., w_k)w=(w1​,w2​,w3​,...,wk​)
According to chain rule:
p(w)=p(w1)p(w2∣w1)p(w3∣w1,w2)...p(wk∣w1...wk−1)p(\bm{w})=p(w_1)p(w_2|w_1)p(w_3|w_1,w_2)...p(w_k|w_1...w_{k-1})p(w)=p(w1​)p(w2​∣w1​)p(w3​∣w1​,w2​)...p(wk​∣w1​...wk−1​)
This can however be simplified with Markov property which states that we only care about up to nnn previous steps when computing the probability of the occurrence of the current state:
p(wi∣w1...wi−1)≈p(wi−n+1...wi−1)p(w_i|w_1...w_{i-1}) \approx p(w_{i-n+1}...w_{i-1})p(wi​∣w1​...wi−1​)≈p(wi−n+1​...wi−1​)
This in scope of a bigram language model, by applying Markov's property, using n=2n=2n=2 we get:
Toy corpus:

  • This is the malt
  • That lay in the house that Jack built
    p(this is the house)=p(this∣Start)p(is∣this)p(the∣is)p(house∣the)p(End∣House)p(\textsf{this is the house})=p(\textsf{this}|\textbf{Start})p(\textsf{is}|\textsf{this})p(\textsf{the}|\textsf{is})p(\textsf{house}|\textsf{the})p(\textbf{End}|\textsf{House})p(this is the house)=p(this∣Start)p(is∣this)p(the∣is)p(house∣the)p(End∣House)
    The special Start and End points are added to the sentence to ensure correct normalization of sentences of different lengths. Putting all of these together, we have the formalised Markov likelihood equation:
    L=p(w)=∏i=1k+1p(wi∣wi−n+1i−1)\mathcal{L} = p(\bm{w})=\prod_{i=1}^{k+1}p(w_i|w_{i-n+1}^{i-1})L=p(w)=i=1∏k+1​p(wi​∣wi−n+1i−1​)
    With estimated probabilities being the count of occurrences of ensemble including the current term divided by the occurrences of ensemble excluding the current term:
    p(wi∣wi−n+1i−1)=c(wi−n+1,...,wi−1,wi)c(wi−n+1,...,wi−1)=c(wi−n+1i)c(wi−n+1i−1)p(w_i|w_{i-n+1}^{i-1}) = \dfrac{c(w_{i-n+1},...,w_{i-1}, w_i)}{c(w_{i-n+1},...,w_{i-1})} = \dfrac{c(w_{i-n+1}^{i})}{c(w_{i-n+1}^{i-1})}p(wi​∣wi−n+1i−1​)=c(wi−n+1​,...,wi−1​)c(wi−n+1​,...,wi−1​,wi​)​=c(wi−n+1i−1​)c(wi−n+1i​)​
    As it turned out, equation above is the solution of the log-likelihood maximization of the Markov equation above.

Perplexity is a measurement of how well a probability model predicts a sample. It may be used to compare probability models. A low perplexity indicates the probability distribution is good at predicting a sample. It is defined as:

P=p(Wtest)−1/N\mathcal{P} =p(\bm{W_{\textsf{test}}})^{-1/N}P=p(Wtest​)−1/N
Where NNN is the length of the test corpus (all words concatenated). Intuitively, it normalizes the likelihood with how long the actual text is, so that low likelihood due to long text will be of higher quality than slightly higher likelihood but with much shorter text.

Smoothing Techniques:

Sometimes, we may end up with infinite perplexity even when we have a reasonable set of testing and training data, for example, given the training corpus: This is the house that Jack Built and the test corpus This is Jack, we have:
p(Jack∣is)=c(is Jack)c(is)=0p(\textsf{Jack}|\textsf{is}) = \dfrac{c(\textsf{is Jack})}{c(\textsf{is})}=0p(Jack∣is)=c(is)c(is Jack)​=0
This will results in L=0\mathcal{L}=0L=0 therefore the perplexity P=inf⁡\mathcal{P}= \infP=inf. In order to combat this, several smoothing techniques are applied.

Laplacian Smoothing

Idea: pull some probability from frequent bigrams to infrequent ones, either just add 1 to the counts (add one smoothing):
p^(wi∣wi−n+1i−1)=c(wi−n+1i)+1c(wi−n+1i−1)+V\hat{p}(w_i|w_{i-n+1}^{i-1}) = \dfrac{c(w_{i-n+1}^{i})+1}{c(w_{i-n+1}^{i-1})+V}p^​(wi​∣wi−n+1i−1​)=c(wi−n+1i−1​)+Vc(wi−n+1i​)+1​
where VVV is the total vocabulary/term size (i.e. unique n-grams within text ensemble). In order to gain more control over the effect of smoothing, we can tune a parameter (add-k smoothing):
p^(wi∣wi−n+1i−1)=c(wi−n+1i)+kc(wi−n+1i−1)+kV\hat{p}(w_i|w_{i-n+1}^{i-1}) = \dfrac{c(w_{i-n+1}^{i})+k}{c(w_{i-n+1}^{i-1})+kV}p^​(wi​∣wi−n+1i−1​)=c(wi−n+1i−1​)+kVc(wi−n+1i​)+k​

Katz Backoff

Longer n-grams are better, but data is not always enough. Hence we try to use longer n-grams, when it fails we simply back off to a shorter one:

p^(wi∣wi−n+1i−1)={pˉ(wi∣wi−n+1i−1)⟺c(wi−n+1i)>0α(wi−n+1i−1)p^(wi∣wi−n+2i−1), otherwise\hat{p}(w_i|w_{i-n+1}^{i-1}) = \begin{cases} \bar{p}(w_i|w_{i-n+1}^{i-1}) & \iff c(w_{i-n+1}^{i})>0\\ \alpha (w_{i-n+1}^{i-1}) \hat{p}(w_i|w_{i-n+2}^{i-1}) & \textrm{, otherwise} \end{cases} p^​(wi​∣wi−n+1i−1​)={pˉ​(wi​∣wi−n+1i−1​)α(wi−n+1i−1​)p^​(wi​∣wi−n+2i−1​)​⟺c(wi−n+1i​)>0, otherwise​

Note the α(wi−n+1i−1)\alpha (w_{i-n+1}^{i-1})α(wi−n+1i−1​) is simply the normalizing constant term used to ensure that the p^\hat{p}p^​ is bounded between 0 and 1.

Interpolation Smoothing

Idea: similar to Katz Backoff method, just make p^\hat{p}p^​ a superposition of different n-gram probabilities:
p^(wi∣wi−n+1i−1)=λ1p(wi∣wi−2,wi−1)+λ2p(wi∣wi−1)+λ3p(wi)\hat{p} ( w_i | w_{i-n+1}^{i-1} ) = \lambda_1 p( w_i | w_{i-2}, w_{i-1} ) + \lambda_2 p( w_i | w_{i-1} ) + \lambda_3 p( w_i )p^​(wi​∣wi−n+1i−1​)=λ1​p(wi​∣wi−2​,wi−1​)+λ2​p(wi​∣wi−1​)+λ3​p(wi​)

Where the parameters must satisfy λ1+λ2+λ3=1\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \lambda_3 = 1λ1​+λ2​+λ3​=1. The weights are optimized on a test set. optionally they can also depend on the context.

Kneser-Ney Smoothing

From the concept of absolute discounting, we can write the conditional probability of the current term given its previous term to be an "interpolation" of the unigram offset-ed by some number:

p^(wi∣wi−1)=λ(wi−1)p(wi)+c(wi−1,wi)−d∑xc(wi−1,x)\hat{p} ( w_i | w_{i-1}) = \lambda(w_{i-1}) p(w_i) + \dfrac{c(w_{i-1}, w_i) - d } {\sum_x c(w_{i-1}, x)}p^​(wi​∣wi−1​)=λ(wi−1​)p(wi​)+∑x​c(wi−1​,x)c(wi−1​,wi​)−d​
As it turned out, this offset can be represented by the proportion of the bigrams containing the current word over the count of bigrams, this is also offset by a magic number "d" being 0.75 in English. This is termed "Absolute Discounting". Note that within the unigram term, we can do better than just purely using the frequency of the current word, for example:

"Why did Anne get off with another ____"

Within our corpus, the occurrence of "pig" is higher than "guy", but it does not make sense for Anne to shag a pig, therefore we use how likely is the word to appear as a novel continuation defined as:
Pcontinuation(wi)∝∣wi−1:c(wi−1,wi)>0∣P_{\textrm{continuation}}(w_i) \propto |{w_{i-1} : c(w_{i-1}, w_i) > 0}|Pcontinuation​(wi​)∝∣wi−1​:c(wi−1​,wi​)>0∣
Here we are just counting how many times the current word forms a unique bigram with its previous term. In this case, we may then select "guy" over "pig". This may seem like a bad example, but think of weird terms like "Francisco" and how it normally appears after the word "San".

The actual PcontinuationP_{\textrm{continuation}}Pcontinuation​ is simply the normalized version of the equation above:

Pcontinuation(wi)=∣wi−1:c(wi−1,wi)>0∣∑w′∣w′i−1:c(w′i−1,w′i)>0∣P_{\textrm{continuation}}(w_i) = \dfrac{|{w_{i-1} : c(w_{i-1}, w_i) > 0}|} {\sum_{\mathcal{w'}}|{\mathcal{w'}_{i-1} : c(\mathcal{w'}_{i-1}, \mathcal{w'}_i) > 0}|}Pcontinuation​(wi​)=∑w′​∣w′i−1​:c(w′i−1​,w′i​)>0∣∣wi−1​:c(wi−1​,wi​)>0∣​
Putting all these together, we have:
p^(wi∣wi−1)=λ(wi−1)Pcontinuation(wi)+c(wi−1,wi)−d∑xc(wi−1,x)\hat{p} ( w_i | w_{i-1}) = \lambda(w_{i-1}) P_{\textrm{continuation}}(w_i) + \dfrac{c(w_{i-1}, w_i) - d } {\sum_x c(w_{i-1}, x)}p^​(wi​∣wi−1​)=λ(wi−1​)Pcontinuation​(wi​)+∑x​c(wi−1​,x)c(wi−1​,wi​)−d​
which is the Kneser-Ney smoothing, it is widely considered the most effective method of smoothing.

Hidden Markov Model

One major usage of NLP is text tagging, this is also used for text generation. The idea is, given a sequence of input text "Isn't it funny" we wish to generate the next most probable text. To do this however, we first map the input sequence to a most probable sequence of tags, we then use this to predict what the next most likely text is. Probabilistic Neural Language Model (Legacy)

First, we represent each word within the text as a vector (using BOM, TF-IDF,etc). Then the input feature vector is simply the n-grams vectors concatenated together:
x=[C(wi−n+1),...,C(wi−1)]Tx = [C(w_{i-n+1}), ..., C(w_{i-1})]^Tx=[C(wi−n+1​),...,C(wi−1​)]T
For example, to produce outputs for the sentence Anne is a horrible person using 3-grams approach we have the first input being x0=[C(Anne),C(is)]Tx_0 = [C(\textsf{Anne}), C(\textsf{is})]^Tx0​=[C(Anne),C(is)]T.

Next, we have the output yyy (tags, vocabulary,etc) being a linear combination of both the raw input feature and the non-linear transformed input feature through some hidden layer:
y=b+Wx+Utanh⁡(d+Hx)y =b + Wx + U\tanh (d+Hx)y=b+Wx+Utanh(d+Hx)
We can interpret WWW as "literal" interpretation from the previous two words, HHH being the "context" provided by the two words, UUU being the "literal" interpretation of our learnt "concept".

Finally, we transfer the output yyy into a probability distribution of different tags/vocabulary outputs. This is a simple softmax transformation:
p(wi∣wi−n+1,...,wi−1)=exp⁡ywi∑w∈Vexp⁡(yw)p(w_i| w_{i-n+1}, ..., w_{i-1}) =\dfrac{\exp y_{w_i}}{\sum_{w \in V} \exp (y_w)}p(wi​∣wi−n+1​,...,wi−1​)=∑w∈V​exp(yw​)expywi​​​

Dimensional analysis: [y]=[b]=∣V∣[y] = [b] = |V|[y]=[b]=∣V∣ where ∣V∣|V|∣V∣ is the size of the vocabulary. [W]=∣V×m∗(n−1)∣[W] = |V \times m*(n-1)|[W]=∣V×m∗(n−1)∣ where mmm is the dimension of the vector representation of one word, nnn is the number of grams. The problem with this method is it is overly complicated.

Log-Bilinear Language Model

After studying the probabilistic neural language model in the previous section, we can effectively simplify the the learnt parameters of WWW and HHH through a simple step. Note since the softmax function is intrinsically non-linear, we also do not need the tanh function.

let y=r^Trwi+bwiy = \hat{r}^Tr_{w_i}+b_{w_i}y=r^Trwi​​+bwi​​, where r^\hat{r}r^ is the context representation of the n-grams, rrr represents the literal representation rwi=C(wi)Tr_{w_i}=C(w_i)^Trwi​​=C(wi​)T of the current word. Putting everything together, yyy is simply the dot product between the literal and context representation of the n-gram, transform this into probability distribution using softmax we have:

Obviously now we only have parameters WWW and bbb to learn. Which is effectively 1/31/31/3 of the computational complexity compared with method described in the previous section.

Vector Space Models of Semantics

In order to measure how similar two words are, we use second order co-occurrences termed paradigmatic parallels.

For example, how do we know that KFC and McDonald's are similar? We take its surrounding nearest neighbours i.e for KFC its most frequent neighbours may be burgers, chicken, nuggets, coke, etc. Each with their own probability of occurrence. We would expect this distrubution to be similar to one for McDonald's.

This section devises different methods on mapping the semantics similarity of two wards onto a vector space.

SVD On Point Mutual Information

Let two words for which their semantics is compared be denoted as WiW_iWi​ and WjW_jWj​. Their features are represented as their nnn most frequent neighbouring words: Wi:{ui−n,...,ui+n}n≠0W_i: \{u_{i-n}, ..., u_{i+n}\} n\neq 0Wi​:{ui−n​,...,ui+n​}n≠0. i.e. for the word KFC the 3 most frequent words to the right may be is, a, fast. To its left may be resturant, food, like, therefore the feature for KFC is {resturant, food, like, is, a, fast}

One classic way of measuring the similarity of two words' semantics is through Point Mutual Information (PMI), measured as:

Skip-gram Model (Word2Vec)

This section aims to learn the "context" (ec\bm{e_c}ec​) of a word x\bm{x}x through learning the transformation matrix Θ\ThetaΘ, in otder to achieve this, we map a one hot encoded word x\bm{x}x to a contect y\bm{y}y which is a probability distribution of all vocabularies. i.e. y=[p(car),p(glass),p(juice),...,p(winter)]T\bm{y} = [p(\textsf{car}), p(\textsf{glass}), p(\textsf{juice}), ..., p(\textsf{winter})]^Ty=[p(car),p(glass),p(juice),...,p(winter)]T for xi=orange\bm{x_i} = \textsf{orange}xi​=orange, yi=[0.04,0.13,0.14,0,0,...,0]T\bm{y_i}=[0.04, 0.13, 0.14, 0, 0, ..., 0]^Tyi​=[0.04,0.13,0.14,0,0,...,0]T. But we are not interested in y\bm{y}y, it is only used in trainign, all we want to know it the weights of the hidden layer!

This model forms the foundation of Word2Vec algorithm which maps a input text xxx to a target yyy in the form of either class labels or related words. The algorithm is trained on a large corpus. For example: we wish to input time and output words such as zone.
Let's say that we are given a sentence "I want a glass of orange juice to go along with my cereal", we pick a random context word orange, we set up a supervised learning model which maps the one hot encoded word x\bm{x}x (i.e. orange) to a range of targets words within a window (say of size 5), i.e. glass, juice, along, cereal.
In order to achieve this, we use one hidden and one output layer to achieve this, denoting hidden layer output as ece_cec​ (context):
ec=Θxe_c=\Theta xec​=Θx
For each output, we have its corresponding weights being Φt\Phi_tΦt​, we use softmax to convert output of the output layer into a probability distribution:
p(y∣x)=eΦyTec∑j=1∣V∣eΦjTecp(y|\bm{x})=\dfrac{e^{\Phi_y^T e_c}}{\sum_{j=1}^{|V|}e^{\Phi_j^T e_c}}p(y∣x)=∑j=1∣V∣​eΦjT​ec​eΦyT​ec​​
All parameters in matrices Θ\ThetaΘ and PhiPhiPhi are learnt through backpropogation using the cross entropy loss function:
L(y^,y)=−∑i=1∣V∣yilog⁡y^i\mathcal{L}(\bm{\hat{y}, y)} = -\sum_{i=1}^{|V|}\bm{y_i\log \hat{y}_i}L(y^​,y)=−i=1∑∣V∣​yi​logy^​i​
Note this is very easy to compute as the target vector yiy_iyi​ is one hot encoded hence the summation is more like a fake sum as there is only non zero value.
As softmax is very inefficient to compute, either more efficient storage system like huffman tree is used, or, more commonly, a Skip-gram Negative Sampling (SGNS) method is used. The idea behind it is we can greatly reduce the number of output vector space by limiting it to a length of 2, i.e. instead of predicting a word for another word, we compute yes or no for word pairs, trained on examples of word pairs vvv and non word pairs (\bar{v}), we use sigmoid function σ\sigmaσ to produce the logistic regression output, we wish the maximize the function below:.
∑u∈W∑v∈Wnu,vlog⁡σ(⟨Φu,Θv⟩)+kE(vˉ)log⁡σ(−⟨Φu,Θvˉ⟩)\sum_{u\in W} \sum_{v \in W} n_{u,v} \log \sigma(\langle \Phi_u, \Theta_v\rangle) + k\mathbb{E}(\bar{v})\log\sigma(-\langle \Phi_u, \Theta_{\bar{v}}\rangle)u∈W∑​v∈W∑​nu,v​logσ(⟨Φu​,Θv​⟩)+kE(vˉ)logσ(−⟨Φu​,Θvˉ​⟩)
Where kkk is the number of samples taken in the negative examples. Note that the whole expectation and kkk bussiness are literally mathematical notations, in reality, for each training step, we just select at random a negative example and feed it into the evaluation network. Probabilistic Latent Semantic Analysis (PLSA)

This is the most basic method for discovering topics within a corpus. We can marginalise the probability distribution of words within a document p(w∣d)p(w|d)p(w∣d) into ∑t∈Tp(w∣t,d)p(t∣d)\sum_{t\in T}p(w|t, d)p(t|d)∑t∈T​p(w∣t,d)p(t∣d), we then assume that topics are independent of documents and finally write down:
p(w∣d)=∑t∈Tp(w∣t)p(t∣d)p(w|d) =\sum_{t\in T}p(w|t)p(t|d)p(w∣d)=t∈T∑​p(w∣t)p(t∣d)
In matrix form, we think of each topic Φt\Phi_tΦt​ being a probability distribution of words in our vocabulary with matrix Φ\PhiΦ with dimension ∣V∣×∣T∣|V|\times |T|∣V∣×∣T∣ (numbers of vocabulary and topics) representing the probability distribution of words over all topics. Each document Θd\Theta_dΘd​ consists of a probability distribution of topics, matrix Θ\ThetaΘ with dimension ∣T∣×∣D∣|T|\times |D|∣T∣×∣D∣ (numbers of topics and documents) representing the probability distribution of topics over documents. We now have:
p(w∣d)=∑t∈TΦwtΘtdp(w|d)=\sum_{t\in T}\Phi_{wt}\Theta_{td}p(w∣d)=t∈T∑​Φwt​Θtd​
Or in matrix multiplication form the matrix X\bm{X}X representing the probability distribution of words in all documents:
X=ΦΘ\bm{X}=\Phi \ThetaX=ΦΘ
Note Θ\ThetaΘ contains information on the distribution of words over each topic (vectorized topics), and Θ\ThetaΘ represents the distribution of topics over each document.

There are two methods to find the two matrices, one may use log-liklihood optimization. Firstly, we compute the probability of generating the completed version of our corpus of documents:
p(D)=∏d∈Dp(d)∏w∈dp(w∣d)nwdp(D) = \prod_{d\in D} p(d) \prod_{w\in d} p(w|d)^{n_{wd}}p(D)=d∈D∏​p(d)w∈d∏​p(w∣d)nwd​
Note the exponent nwdn_{wd}nwd​ denotes the number of occurrences of the word www within the document ddd, obviously as we are expecting nwdn_{wd}nwd​ occurrences within the document, the total probability of the word being formed within the document should multiply each time it is generated.
Substituting the matrix representation of p(w∣d)p(w|d)p(w∣d) in and taking the log, we obtain the log-liklihood optimization of PLSA model:
log⁡(∏d∈Dp(d)∏w∈d(∑t∈TΦwtΘtd)ndw)→max⁡Φ,Θ\log \: \left(\prod_{d\in D} p(d) \prod_{w\in d}(\sum_{t\in T}\Phi_{wt}\Theta_{td})^{n_{dw}}\right) \rightarrow \max_{\Phi, \Theta}log(d∈D∏​p(d)w∈d∏​(t∈T∑​Φwt​Θtd​)ndw​)→Φ,Θmax​
Assuming uniform distribution of documents p(d)p(d)p(d), the expression above can be simplified into:
∑d∈D∑w∈Dnwdlog⁡∑t∈TΦwtΘtd→max⁡Φ,Θ\sum_{d\in D} \sum_{w\in D} n_{wd}\log \sum_{t\in T}\Phi_{wt}\Theta_{td} \rightarrow \max_{\Phi, \Theta}d∈D∑​w∈D∑​nwd​logt∈T∑​Φwt​Θtd​→Φ,Θmax​
Note that we have constraints on these variables that need to optimized, since they cannot be negative and must be bounded to a probability distribution:

{Φwt≥0&Θtd≥0∑w∈WΦwt=1&∑t∈TΘtd=1\begin{cases} \Phi_{wt}\geq 0 \qquad \qquad \& \qquad \Theta_{td} \geq 0 \\ \\ \sum_{w\in W}\Phi_{wt}=1 \; \; \; \; \& \qquad \sum_{t\in T}\Theta_{td}=1 \end{cases} ⎩⎪⎨⎪⎧​Φwt​≥0&Θtd​≥0∑w∈W​Φwt​=1&∑t∈T​Θtd​=1​

Map VS FlatMap

Use Map for 1:1 relationship between input & output

'WordLengths' >> beam.Map(lambda word: (word, len(word)))

FlatMap for non 1:1 relationships, usually with a generator:

def vowels(word):
  for ch in word:
    if ch in ['a', 'e', 'i', 'o', 'u']:
      yield ch

'WordVowels' >> beam.FlatMap(lambda word: vowels(word))
